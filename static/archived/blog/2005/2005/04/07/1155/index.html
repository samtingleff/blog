The smart money in real estate

It seems that the smart money (just private, local real-estate investors) "were the biggest net sellers of property last year, selling off about $4.5 billion more than they purchased."

Good news for those of us with dreams of actually owning a house in California some day.

Posted by sam at April 7, 2005 01:21 PM

Comments

In some places, the real estate market is already crashing, e.g., Las Vegas. Housing prices have been falling there, even for new houses. One recent home buyer (a Californian who bought a house for investment, not as a residence) sued a developer because the developer dropped the prices on new but unsold houses to below what the investor had recently paid for his house. A $100,000 drop in price really hurt. Ouch!

California real estate prices are still going up, but I think that the current financing mechanism may put a brake on this. The vast majority of people here are now putting down very small down payments, if any, and are getting adjustable rate mortgages that they can barely afford now. As interest rates rise, the mortgage payments will rise, and some people won't be able to cover the new monthly payments. Then, there will be relative glut of homes for sale, driving down home prices. That's just a guess.

However, I know people who have already experienced the problem. One Indian engineer told me that he was moving out of a big home in the hills of Fremont overlooking San Francisco Bay. He had just purchased a small lot and a double-wide trailer in Gilroy for his family. There was only one problem. He had not told his wife about this impending change. Can anybody give me the Hindi translations of the following terms: down scaling, unrealistic expectations, disappointment, trailer, garlic fields, asshole?

Peace!

I agree, Sam. Even though homes are sitting on the market a bit longer (in San Diego), and the volume of sales has decreased slightly, we are still seeing appreciation well into the double digits year over year, when RE experts have been predicting appreciation to drop into the single digits for a year or two now.

Now some say that because the Affordability Index for homes is 11%, i.e. only 11% of families can technically afford the median priced home in San Diego, the bubble will have to burst. But, I think the consensus here is that this is not expected to burst any bubbles, but that appreciation may decelerate (again, they've been saying this since 2003). In other words, there are very few people who think that prices will actually drop in real terms.

And with a built up demand (we're over 100,000 housing units short here) and continued competition, it's even harder to see why any bubble would burst.

Still, it's a social problem, too. When your teachers, police officers and firefighters can't afford to live in the cities they serve, it's a sad imbalance and one wonders if this situation can or should be sustained.
