I voted

After seeing my polling place today I am glad to know that we have OSCE observers monitoring the process.

My "ward", which I assume is just some fraction of the city of Ithaca, is divided into two districts. My polling place had one line for each district, with a map and a person to help people figure out which district they are in. Of course everybody is in my district, leaving one of the two voting machines and 50% of the workers idle while the other line nears 30 people.

As this line backed up, the two older women with the voter registration books showed no sign of hurry. Not only were they not hurrying, they were some of the slowest moving people I've ever seen.

Imagine the scene like this:
------- line of people ------------- [voter sign-in] - [voting booth]
The [voting booth] only holds one person at a time and clearly is the bottleneck. With 20+ people waiting in front of the [voter sign-in], you might think they would add a buffer of even one person between the [voter sign-in] and the [voting booth] to ensure that the [voting booth] (which is the bottleneck, remember?) operates at 100% capacity. No. The [voting booth] appears to be idle about 30% of the time because the two older women at the [voter sign-in] are so slow and do not recognize the need for a buffer.

We were joking around in line about how they might actually slow the process even further. When... some guy in an NRA hat starts complaining that the voting machine is broken. Fortunately he was referring to the other machine but we had to laugh.

Oh yeah - it sucks to know that my vote doesn't count for anything. That's so stooopid.
