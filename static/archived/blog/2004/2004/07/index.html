July 23, 2004

I met Scoble at BlogOn 2004

And I came within six inches of Craig from Craigslist fame. There's a summer highlight for you.

Today I'm at the BlogOn 2004 conference on a student scholarship. I'm here as an investor, blogger and shill for SightSpeed.

But if I hear the damn word 'blog' one more time I'm going to run. Something about that word has always bugged me... This whole conference strikes me as a real in-crowd congratulatory "look at all the cool stuff we're doing, oh we're so great; we're going to revolutionize media - blah blah blah." A bunch of smart people focusing on problems that really don't matter all that much.

I think that's what bugs me about 'blog.' Like most jargon it seems designed to differentiate those who know and those who don't. And everybody here 'knows' and the tone of all the talk strikes me as self-congratulatory about knowing.

Early in the day someone even showed a video of short on-the-street interviews in NYC. They asked random people if they know "what blogging is." Most didn't of course, and the whole crowd laughed. Was the laughter at 'us' or 'them'? That was unclear.

Unlike any other conference I've ever been to, when I introduced myself as a Cornell student and intern I kept getting the same feeling, like they were making an instant judgement that I'm a nobody. Eyes would glaze over and their attention was long gone. Never had that experience before.

July 21, 2004

The buying funnel

If you've been paying attention (don't worry if you haven't), you know that I learned all of three things in school last year (1, 2, 3). I've learned a few things about startup marketing this summer and I'm going to begin sharing them now. First up, the funnel.

Always know your buying funnel. The funnel is the process a customer goes through to actually buy something. For SightSpeed, the process looks something like this (each stage could even be broken down further):

  1. Hear about the product
  2. Visit the web site
  3. Register
  4. Install software
  5. Confirm registration
  6. Try software
  7. Buy service

If you know exactly how many people you lose at each stage you can 1) benchmark against industry norms, 2) benchmark against yourself over time and 3) better determine where to allocate resources.

Next up, the power of low expectations.
