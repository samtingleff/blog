Question: Early writing and printing technologies differ from recent digital media methods. Do new technologies qualitatively change the publishing process?

New digital publishing techniques qualitatively change the publishing process. Digital publishing has created entirely new forms of information with no analog counterpart. As digital media become dominant, the limitations of paper publishing are only now becoming obvious. Paper limits both the volume of information and its format. Information on paper is by nature unmanipulatable and inflexible. It is a one-way flow from the author to the reader without the possibility of interaction.

Writing and printing techniques limit the type and volume of information available. Books do not allow the database storage and retrieval of digital media. CD-ROM encyclopedias now contain video and audio clips. They can contain sophisticated search engines with instant references. One CD-ROM contains a huge variety of information not accessible in print media.

Digital media also allow real-time interaction with the reader, creating an interactive environment not possible with traditional publishing methods. What Forrester Research (1997) describes as "Transactive Content" is an example of this. It is content and transaction driven by input from the reader. Web sites take cues from the reader and tailor content and transactions to the reader's perceived needs and desires. This interaction is an entirely new form of mass communication.

Yahoo's map database is a another good example of a format not possible with analog media. On a user query it will display a map of anywhere in the country. Microsoft's TerraServer contains satellite images of almost everywhere on the planet. The analog equivalent to this page would require huge amounts of paper and would be extremely slow to search. It would be spatially located, limiting access to a small audience. The digital version is quick and accessible around the world. This difference represents a qualitative change from traditional print media.

Digital publishing also offers three dimensional imaging and manipulation not available in print media. Organic molecules can be modeled, displayed, rotated, and manipulated in ways not possible with analog media. The University of Texas at Austin Botany Department has a "space filling model of a glucan chain of cellulose" on their web page. This model could be made physically, but could not be published to a large audience.

The San Diego Supercomputer Center has various two and three dimensional models of the San Diego Bay. These digital models of complex biological systems are possible only with powerful computational resources. They are available to a large audience on the World Wide Web only because of advances in digital communication technologies. In an article for Issues in Science and Technology Librarianship Vincent H. Resh (1998) says that electronic publishing allows a variety of information not available with traditional media. He describes an on-line journal New Astronomy with a model of "how one star sucks up matter from the other." This complex visual imaging and manipulation is possible only with the advent of digital publishing, and it represents a qualitative change from the static content of traditional print media.
