Question: Emerging communication technologies have already changed the commercial landscape. Discuss how digital technologies have and continue to change how individuals and organizations conduct business.

Digital technologies have and will continue to change the business world. They are changing the nature of business transactions by eliminating layers of mediation between producer and consumer. This shortening of the supply chain has changed the types and number of available jobs. Digital communications have changed the costs associated with start-up businesses. Digital storage, retrieval, and communication methods are also changing marketing techniques.

Digital communication protocols such as the World Wide Web, electronic mail, and FTP reduce the communication distance between supply and demand. Traditional retail outlets require managers, salespeople, and distribution networks. The web reduces all of these to a graphical user interface leading to the producer. Ordering a computer from the Gateway web site is a very different process than going to Fry's for the same product. This change reduces in-store labor costs while it creates new costs of web design, programming, and maintenance. While the costs do not always pay for themselves, companies feel forced to enter the on-line arena to prepare for the mythical time when e-commerce rules the world.

Another aspect of the move to electronic commerce and an information economy is the loss of jobs in less technical fields. Salespeople, marketers, store managers, and parts of the distribution networks are all being replaced by digital technologies (Edupage). Their jobs are replaced by employees skilled in computer software and graphic design, which often require more education and training (Hall,B.C. Ministry of Education). Automation in production, along with the move to a global economy, has eliminated many factory jobs.

The start-up costs associated with an on-line business are entirely different from those of a traditional retailer. For a few thousand dollars in hardware and $20 per month in internet access anyone can have access to a global audience. Establishing and maintaining an e-commerce site, however, requires a degree of technical knowledge that is still rare. With the inevitable improvement of web design software such as Microsoft FrontPage and Macromedia Dreamweaver, the skill required will decrease.

The problem on the web is not a lack of audience, it is a scarcity of attention. Enter a search for "books" at excite.com and a paid banner for amazon.com pops up. Enter "computer software" and a Compaq ad appears. There may an independent bookstore with prices cheaper than amazon.com on the web, but it will take much more effort to find it.

Digital communications have begun to change the marketing industry as well. Large-scale databases allow the collection of minute details about individual spending. Shopping "clubs" like those of Ralph's and Vons, record every item bought by their customers. These records are sold to direct marketers who create detailed consumer profiles. Marketers use the profiles to target consumers with their desired characteristics (Turow). This degree of profiling and target marketing is made possible only by the storage and retrieval functions of large digital databases.

Digital technologies are changing the business and marketing landscape at a rapid pace. New technologies, however, emerge within the political and social climate of the time. In the United States they are appropriated and used within the context of mass production and mass advertising. The subversive and potentially revolutionary qualities of emerging communication methods are subdued by their placement in an economy of commercial media and mass production.
